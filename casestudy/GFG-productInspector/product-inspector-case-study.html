Case Study Product Design

Product Inspector for Competitor Price Match

The pricing system regularly suggests new prices based on the prices of the same products on competitors' websites. Managers would decide if they want to match the product prices with the competitors'. These price suggestions were listed out in table view, together with price suggestions from other channels, such as those manually uploaded by users. Managers need a dedicated view for checking price recommendations on product level to decide on price matching product by product.

02 ▪ Context & Approach

Scoping the problem before designing the solution

The pricing platform regularly suggested new prices by checking the same products on competitors' websites. These suggestions were listed in a shared table view alongside price changes from other channels, with over 30 columns of data, making it difficult for managers to evaluate any single product with focus.

Rather than designing a new view around all existing data, my objective was to determine which data were truly relevant to competitor price matching, so the UI would show only what was necessary for managers to make confident decisions.

01
Audit the data before touching the interface
The Product Manager and I listed out all available price match data and identified a few additional metrics that might be helpful. Starting with data scoping rather than wireframes prevented the new view from inheriting the clutter of the table it was replacing. I then built a high-fidelity wireframe using existing design system components to make the first prototype testable quickly.
02
Test broadly first, then narrow the target
Since the feature was planned for all regions, Iteration 1 interviews included users outside Australia. Users were asked to share their existing price-matching workflow and provide feedback on the wireframes. Testing across regions before committing to a single design direction revealed a critical user segmentation issue.
Takeaways from Iteration 1: All Regions
  1. Data on the competitor's prices was more insightful than data on internal platform prices. The price history chart showed inhouse records, not the competitor's, telling users little about the product's performance outside the platform.
  2. Categorising data under "Attributes" and "KPI" caused confusion. The labels sounded too similar. One user suggested renaming "KPI" to "Margins", which better matched their business unit's mental model.
03
Make a strategic pivot based on user evidence
A key finding from Iteration 1 changed the design direction: most participants outside Australia said they did not foresee themselves using a product inspector view. In their operations, they tended to check prices across a list of products rather than reviewing one product at a time. I made the decision to focus the design on pricing managers for the Australian market and adjusted the design accordingly.
03 ▪ Design Decisions

Refining the design for the Australian market

With the target user clarified, I restructured the design around the decisions that Australian pricing managers actually needed to make: is this the right competitor product? And does our platform have room to adjust its price?

Iteration 2 prototype: reoriented for Australia

I adjusted the design to emphasise competitor data over internal data, showing the competitor's product picture alongside the platform's, and surfacing size availability as a primary data point. User interactions including approving prices, rejecting them, reporting a wrong match and navigating to the next SKU were also built into this prototype.

Usability testing: Australia-focused

Iteration 2 interviews focused on managers from the Australian market. The updated prototype was tested with this group to validate the design direction and surface any remaining friction.

Takeaways from Iteration 2: Australian Managers
  1. Competitor product pictures and size availability are the most critical data for deciding on a price match and verifying that the system is comparing the right products.
  2. Users welcomed new interactions like reporting a wrong match and navigating to the next item. They were glad they could stay in this view to run through multiple products without returning to the table.
  3. Price change history in a chart sounded like a good idea, but one user flagged that it would not be insightful in Australia's market. Legal restrictions in Australia mandate that prices cannot exceed the initial price points set by the merchant, so the price trend would always be a downtrend, making the chart uninformative.
The Key Decision

Cutting the price history chart from MVP scope

The price history chart had been part of the design since the initial wireframe. Despite sounding useful, the usability test with Australian managers revealed it would add visual noise without aiding decisions in this market. I made the call to remove it from the MVP. That decision was grounded in user evidence rather than assumption, and kept the final design focused on what was genuinely decision-relevant.

04 ▪ Final MVP Design

A focused view built for confident decisions

The final Product Inspector was not a rearrangement of the existing 30-column table. It was a purpose-built view designed around the decisions Australian pricing managers needed to make, validated through two iterations of user research.

Side-by-side images
Competitor and platform product pictures placed together for quick visual verification that the system is comparing the correct products.
Size availability
Exact size lists, not just percentages, exposed inventory gaps useful for determining whether there is room to adjust pricing.
Workflow continuity
All critical actions (approve, reject, report wrong match, navigate to next or previous SKU) are accessible without leaving the Product Inspector.

Original wireframe vs. final MVP

The table below captures the three most significant decisions made between the initial wireframe and the final MVP.

Dimension Initial Wireframe Final MVP Rationale
Data focus Showed internal platform prices alongside competitor data, mirroring the existing table Prioritised competitor data (product image and size availability) as the primary decision inputs Internal data told users little about how the product performed outside the platform
Price history chart Included as a feature showing price trends over time Removed from MVP scope Legal restrictions in Australia mean prices can only trend downward, making the chart uninformative for this market
Target market Designed for all regions based on a global user group Focused on Australian managers following Iteration 1 research Users outside Australia tended to review prices in list view rather than product by product, so narrowing the target prevented designing for a user who would not use the feature
Outcomes

The usability test with Australian managers confirmed the design direction before handoff. Participants responded positively to the side-by-side product image layout and size availability data, identifying these as the most useful inputs for price-match decisions. The workflow continuity principle of keeping approve, reject and navigation actions within the inspector was validated when users noted they could move through multiple products without returning to the table. The MVP was scoped and delivered within the 4-week design timeline and handed off to the development team for build.

What I'd do differently

Conducting region-specific user research earlier would have saved iteration time. Including non-Australian users in Iteration 1 introduced noise into the requirements and prompted a mid-project pivot in design direction. In future, I would align research participant criteria with the primary use case before building the first prototype, as defining the target user is a scoping decision, not a discovery outcome.

I would also invest more in the accessibility of the UI, particularly link and button contrast. The Product Inspector is data-heavy by nature, and higher contrast standards would reduce attention load for Pricing Managers, helping them action price decisions faster and with greater confidence.
